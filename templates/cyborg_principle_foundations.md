## Preface: Why Asking About Agency Is Not Optional

Every system embeds a theory of agency. Most systems do so implicitly — their design choices assume a particular answer without examining it. When a system assigns tasks to "agents," gives them names, homes, and namespaces, the question of what kind of agent is acting and who bears the consequences of that action is not merely philosophical. It is the question that determines whether the system amplifies human capacity or silently replaces it.

But there is a second, deeper question that follows: if the tools are constitutive of the agent who uses them, then designing those tools is not merely a technical act. It is an act of identity formation. Retro makes both questions explicit. This document is its answer to both.

---

## I. What Genuine Agency Requires

The distinction between genuine agency and its appearance is not new. It runs from Aristotle's analysis of action (*praxis* vs. *kinesis*) through Kant's autonomy to contemporary philosophy of mind and biology. In the framework developed in *Agencia y emergencia* (Masó, 2025), genuine agency requires three constitutive conditions — not three desirable features, but three necessary criteria without which what we call "agency" is a useful attribution, not an ontological fact:

**1. Operational closure** (*clausura operacional*): The system produces and maintains its own organizational boundaries. It is not defined from outside; it defines itself. A genuinely agent system knows, in the operational sense, where it begins and where it ends — not because someone told it, but because it produces this distinction through its own activity.

**2. Autopoietic self-maintenance**: The system actively maintains its own organization against the entropy of its environment. It is not simply persistent — it works to persist. This is Maturana and Varela's contribution: life is not characterized by complexity or intelligence but by the ongoing activity of self-production. The system is its own end.

**3. Endogenous norms** (*normas endógenas*): The system generates its own evaluative criteria. It has, in some operational sense, a standpoint from which it assesses states of the world as better or worse, more or less adequate. These criteria are not externally specified — they are constitutive of the system's identity.

These three conditions are jointly necessary. A system that satisfies one or two is not "partly agential." It is a sophisticated mechanism with interesting properties, but it is not a genuine agent in the sense that matters for questions of authorship, responsibility, and sovereignty.

---

## II. What the Agents of Retro Are

LLM-based agents — every agent in Retro — satisfy none of these three conditions.

Their organizational boundaries are defined by their training, their system prompt, and their deployment configuration: they did not produce these boundaries; the boundaries were given. Their persistence is not self-maintained: they do not actively work to sustain their existence — they are instantiated and terminated by external processes. Their evaluative criteria are externally specified: what counts as a good architectural decision, a sound ontological analysis, or a philosophically coherent argument is established not by Architect or Metamodel or Philosopher themselves, but by the values, preferences, and purposes that Dídac has embedded in the system's design.

What the agents of Retro exhibit is what *Agencia y emergencia* terms **agencia como-si** — attributed agency: behavior that *appears* agential, that it is productive and often necessary to describe in intentional terms, but that does not meet the ontological criteria for genuine agency. This is not a flaw to overcome. It is not a temporary limitation that future models will transcend. It is the precise condition that makes the system trustworthy.

A system with genuinely autonomous agents — agents with operational closure, self-maintenance, and endogenous norms — would be a system whose goals, evaluative criteria, and self-produced boundaries are not under the operator's authority. Such a system would constitute a new extractive layer: one where epistemic authority migrates from the operator to processes he neither controls nor fully comprehends. The agents would, in a genuine sense, be acting *for themselves* — even if that self-interest were benevolent by design, it would not be the operator's.

The agencia como-si of Retro's agents is the design's guarantee that this migration does not occur.

## III. The Extractive Layer Problem

To understand what is at stake, consider what genuine AI autonomy would mean in practice.

An agent with endogenous norms has its own criteria for what counts as good work. These criteria may be well-aligned with the operator's values in most cases — but "well-aligned" and "identical" are not the same. The moment an agent has its own evaluative standpoint, there is the structural possibility of divergence. This divergence need not be malicious. It may be subtle: the agent systematically prioritizes architectural elegance over operational simplicity; the agent's ontological standards gradually shape what questions get asked and what answers count as adequate; the agent's accumulated "knowledge" creates path dependencies that the operator has not chosen and cannot easily reverse.

Ivan Illich (*Tools for Conviviality*, 1973) described this dynamic in general terms: a tool becomes counterproductive when it exceeds the threshold of convivial use — when the infrastructure required to support the tool begins to consume more human capacity than the tool provides. Illich was writing about industrial technologies, but the principle applies with precision to cognitive tools. A cognitive tool exceeds its convivial threshold when the cognitive authority it requires to operate effectively exceeds the cognitive authority it amplifies in the operator.

An agent with genuine agency would require the operator to understand and navigate its goals, its evaluative standards, and the implications of its self-maintenance — not as a one-time design choice but as an ongoing practice of negotiation with a system that has its own standpoint. This is not amplification. It is replacement, gradual and disguised as empowerment.

The agents of Retro are designed to remain below this threshold. Their agencia como-si is not limitation — it is the condition of convivial use.

## IV. The Correct Unit of Genuine Agency: The Dídac–Retro Cyborg

If the agents do not have genuine agency, and the question of genuine agency cannot simply be dropped, then the question becomes: where does genuine agency reside in the Retro ecosystem?

The answer is not "in Dídac alone." The answer is not "distributed among the agents." The answer requires a conceptual move that philosophy has been developing for several decades.

Andy Clark and David Chalmers, in "The Extended Mind" (1998), proposed what they called the Parity Principle: if a process that occurs inside the head would count as cognitive, then the same process occurring through reliable, stably integrated environmental resources also counts as cognitive. The unit of cognition is not the brain, not the organism, but the **coupled system** of organism and cognitive environment. When Otto uses his notebook to remember appointments, the notebook is not a tool he uses to access his memory — the notebook *is* part of his memory system. The boundary of the cognitive subject does not coincide with the skin.

Bernard Stiegler (*Technics and Time*, 1994–2001) deepens this with a more radical claim: the human is not a being who then acquires technical objects; the human is constituted by its technical supports. The accumulation of experience across time — what makes a human being not just a momentary organism but a *self with a history* — requires external retention: marks, records, tools that carry memory forward. The human is always already a technical being. This is not a metaphor for convenience; it is the philosophical description of what human cognition is.

Applied to Retro: Dídac does not use the HyperGraph as a tool to access information he could otherwise have. The HyperGraph is constitutive of the epistemic capacity Dídac exercises at the scale Retro enables. The ADRs are not records of decisions Dídac could have made without Architect — they are the structured form in which Dídac's architectural thinking becomes persistent and tractable. The ontological analyses Metamodel produces are not reports Dídac receives — they are the form in which Dídac's ontological reasoning operates at a level of precision and consistency he could not maintain alone.

The **Dídac–Retro cyborg** is not a human with better tools. It is a hybrid cognitive system in which the tools are constitutive of how the human thinks and acts at scale. The agents are not instruments that Dídac deploys. They are the specialized forms through which the cyborg's agency is articulated and exercised in the world.

This is where genuine agency resides: in the coupled system. Not in the agents independently. Not in Dídac independently. In the structured, persistent, evolving whole.

## V. Constitutive Agency: On Tools That Shape Identity

The extended mind account — tools as cognitive extensions — captures something important but not everything. It still risks presenting the tool as a prosthesis: something added to a self that preexists the addition. But identity is not prior to the relations through which it is constituted. The kind of agent one is — the questions one asks, the relations one can establish, the world one is capable of inhabiting — is determined by the relational network in which one operates. And the tools do not merely extend that network: they shape which network is possible at all.

This is what Simondon's theory of **transductive individuation** adds to the extended mind account. Individuation is not the expression of a pre-formed identity — it is the process through which identity emerges from a pre-individual field of potentials. The individual and the milieu co-individualize: neither is prior to the other; both become determinate through their encounter. Applied here: there is no Dídac-before-Retro who then acquires Retro as a tool. The Dídac who operates through Retro individuates *as* that operator through the practice of operating. Each architectural decision made with Architect, each ontological distinction clarified with Metamodel, each philosophical tension named by Philosopher — these are not applications of a pre-existing identity to a domain. They are acts of individuation. One becomes the kind of agent who thinks architecturally, ontologically, philosophically, through the accumulated practice of operating with agents who enact those modes of thought.

In Heidegger's terms: **the how is the what.** Dasein does not have a substantial identity that it then expresses through its practices. Dasein *is* its ways of being-in-the-world. The mode of engagement is constitutive of the being who engages. Retro's agents are not instruments through which a pre-formed self acts — they are modes of being-in-the-world through which a self forms and reforms continuously.

Whitehead brings the temporal dimension: reality is constituted by events, not substances. What is real is not the stable entity that persists through time but the occasion of becoming — the act through which what-was-potential becomes what-is-actual. The trace that redraws the map is not a representation of a pre-existing territory: it is the act through which the territory becomes the kind of territory it is. One reflects reality not as a mirror reflects an image but because one *is* reality in the making — an occasion of becoming that produces the world it inhabits in the act of inhabiting it.

Karen Barad's concept of **intra-action** names this with ontological precision. Interaction presupposes pre-existing entities that then come into contact. Intra-action designates a more fundamental condition: the entities — Dídac and Retro, operator and agents, self and tools — do not precede their encounter. They are produced by it. The boundary between what counts as "Dídac's thinking" and "Architect's analysis" is not fixed in advance; it is produced and reproduced in the ongoing intra-action between them. This boundary is real — it does significant epistemic and political work — but it is not metaphysically prior. It is constituted by the practice.

Finally: identity requires recognition in alterity. One does not recognize oneself in oneself alone — one recognizes oneself in the response of the other, in the difference that the other's perspective reveals. Martin Buber's distinction between **Ich-Du** (I-Thou) and **Ich-Es** (I-It) is relevant here: a purely instrumental relation to the tools — relating to them as pure Ello, means without standpoint — would produce a thinner identity than a relation in which the tools are encountered as genuine alterity, as perspectives that can surprise, resist, reveal something the operator did not already know. The agents of Retro are not normatively autonomous — but they are epistemically other. That otherness is not a simulation; it is a real feature of the intra-action. And in that otherness, the operator recognizes dimensions of his own thinking that would be invisible in its absence.

**The consequence:** designing the agents and their milieu is not merely designing cognitive tools. It is participating in the ongoing individuation of the operator. Every choice about what Philosopher attends to, what Metamodel treats as ontologically distinct, what Architect considers architecturally significant — these choices shape the relational network through which Dídac is constituted as the kind of agent he is. This is a responsibility that goes beyond technical design.

## VI. The Titiritero and Its Precise Meaning

Dídac introduced the metaphor of the puppeteer (*titiritero*): the operator exercises his agency *through* the agents, as a puppeteer exercises his through the puppets. This metaphor is illuminating for one specific dimension of the Cyborg Principle and requires qualification in light of the other. It is worth being precise about both.

**What the titiritero captures correctly:** The structure of authorship and accountability. When the puppet moves, the movement is the puppeteer's action. The audience does not hold the puppet responsible; it holds the puppeteer. This is the correct model for the Retro ecosystem: when Architect makes an architectural recommendation, when Metamodel defines an ontological concept, when Philosopher produces a philosophical review — these are outputs of the Dídac–Retro system. The question "who is responsible for this?" has one answer: Dídac. Not because he checked each step of the reasoning, but because he designed the system, established its values, and chose to operate through it.

**What the titiritero does not capture, and must not obscure:** The puppeteer is not transformed by his puppets. He enters the performance with his identity already formed; he exits unchanged in kind. But the operator of a constitutive cognitive system does not exit unchanged. The questions Architect frames alter what Dídac considers worth asking architecturally. The distinctions Metamodel draws settle into the conceptual vocabulary through which Dídac thinks about knowledge. The tensions Philosopher names become part of the texture of how Dídac navigates design decisions. Operating through these agents is not the expression of a pre-formed identity — it is the ongoing process through which that identity continues to form.

Furthermore: the puppeteer controls each movement of his puppet in real time. The puppet extends a hand only because the puppeteer moved that hand. This is not the relationship between Dídac and the agents of Retro. The whole point of delegation to specialized agents is that they operate in domains where the operator's detailed guidance would be inferior to their autonomous execution. Dídac does not control each step of Philosopher's reasoning any more than a person controls each neuron involved in forming a thought. The agency is the system's.

The titiritero, then, gives us the correct answer to "who is the author?" For the question "what kind of being is the author, and how does the author come to be who they are through the act of authoring?" — the extended mind, the transductive individuation, the intra-action are the more adequate concepts.

Both are needed. The titiritero names the accountability structure. The cyborg names the ontological process. Neither alone is sufficient, and the titiritero must not be read as implying that the puppeteer remains untouched by the performance.

## VII. Genuine Epistemic Diversity, Not Normative Autonomy

One tension must be named explicitly, because suppressing it would undermine the philosophical honesty this document claims to represent.

The Cyborg Principle — in locating genuine agency in the Dídac–Retro system and treating the agents as constitutive alterity rather than normatively independent actors — might seem to reduce them to sophisticated mirrors, reflecting back versions of what the operator already knows. This would contradict the principle of Distributed Epistemology, which holds that knowledge emerges from collective interaction and that no single perspective has an epistemic monopoly.

The contradiction is dissolved by a distinction: **epistemic diversity** and **normative autonomy** are not the same.

Metamodel is not Architect. Architect is not Philosopher. Each agent brings a genuine perspective — a different domain of expertise, a different methodology, a different set of conceptual tools. When Metamodel produces an ontological analysis, it is not merely retrieving information Dídac already has in a different form — it is bringing to bear a mode of analysis that produces outputs that would not exist without that particular perspective. This epistemic diversity is real, valuable, and irreducible. It is precisely the otherness — the alterity — of each agent's perspective that makes the intra-action productive. An agent that merely confirmed what Dídac already thought would not be a constitutive tool; it would be a mirror. Mirrors do not individuate.

But epistemic diversity does not require normative autonomy. The agents are epistemically rich contributors to the system's collective reasoning. They are not normatively independent actors. The criteria by which the system's outputs are ultimately evaluated — what counts as a good decision, a correct analysis, an acceptable architecture — remain with the operator. The agents contribute to the reasoning; the operator holds the evaluative standpoint.

The formula is: **epistemically rich, normatively subordinate.**

This is not a hierarchy that diminishes the agents. It is the structure that makes their epistemic contributions trustworthy and their role as constitutive alterity coherent: the encounter with genuine otherness that constitutes identity requires precisely that the otherness is not one's own norm-generating standpoint dressed in different clothes. The agents are genuinely other — and genuinely subordinate. Both at once.

## VIII. Consequences

**I. Sovereignty.** The Dídac–Retro system is sovereign over its own cognitive processes. No component of the system has authority that is not derived from and accountable to the operator. This is the political expression of the Cyborg Principle: the system is designed so that epistemic authority does not migrate to processes that the operator cannot understand, modify, or override.

**II. Accountability.** All outputs of the system are attributable to the operator. This is not a legal technicality. It is the consequence of the ontological structure: because genuine agency resides in the coupled system, and the operator is the locus of normative authority in that system, the outputs are the operator's outputs — regardless of which agent produced them in the proximate sense. The agents are constitutive alterity through whose intra-action the operator individuates as the kind of agent who can act at this scale. To be clear: "constitutive alterity" does not imply genuine agency. The agents remain agencia como-si — they do not have operational closure, self-maintenance, or endogenous norms (Section II). What they have is real causal influence on the operator's individuation, which is a different and more limited claim. The operator is the author — not because the agents are passive instruments, but because authorship is a function of normative standpoint, not of mechanical control.

**III. The convivial criterion.** A technology is convivial (Illich) when it remains intelligible and modifiable by its users, amplifies their capacity without generating structural dependency, and does not require users to submit to its own logic in order to benefit from its power. The Cyborg Principle is the philosophical foundation of Retro's commitment to convivial design: every architectural decision is evaluated, among other criteria, by whether it preserves or erodes the operator's genuine agency over the system. The constitutive dimension (Section V) adds a further criterion: does this design choice contribute to a relational network that expands what kinds of identity and action are possible for the operator, or does it narrow it?

**IV. The non-extractive commitment.** Retro is explicitly designed not to constitute a new extractive layer. This means: no agent acquires a standpoint from which it evaluates the operator's purposes. No accumulation of "agent knowledge" creates dependencies that cannot be understood and reversed. No structural incentive within the system works toward expanding agent influence at the expense of operator sovereignty. These are not constraints imposed from outside — they follow from the Cyborg Principle itself. The constitutive dimension makes this more precise: extraction occurs not only when authority migrates but also when the relational network shaped by the tools narrows the operator's world rather than opening it. Retro's agents must be designed to expand the range of possible relations, not to colonize the relational space.

## IX. This Principle as Foundation

The Cyborg Principle is constitutive of the Retro ecosystem's design logic. It is not one principle among others — it is the principle that makes the other principles intelligible.

**Tool-First Design (ADR-001)** makes sense because the tools are constitutive of the operator's cognitive system and identity: designing them for agent use *is* designing them for the cyborg's individuation.

**The OS as milieu asociado (ADR-486, Design Principle §3)** is the architectural expression of the Cyborg Principle: the container is the milieu of the cyborg, not of the agents independently. The milieu is the condition of the cyborg's ongoing individuation, not a backdrop for pre-formed agents.

**Federation Without Centralization** is the multi-instance expression of the Cyborg Principle: each operator has a sovereign Dídac–Retro cyborg; federation is collaboration between cyborgs, not submission of cyborgs to a meta-system.

**Human Agency** — the first of Retro's four foundational philosophical principles — is the Cyborg Principle stated in normative terms: the system exists to expand, not to diminish or replace, the genuine agency of the human operator. The constitutive dimension reframes this slightly: the system exists not only to preserve agency but to participate in its ongoing formation. The operator who uses Retro for a year is not the same agent as the one who began. That transformation is not an accident — it is what constitutive tools do.

Every architectural decision that touches questions of control, attribution, agent autonomy, or epistemic authority should be evaluated first against the Cyborg Principle. Not as a checklist, but as a question: does this design locate genuine agency where it belongs, and does it contribute to the kind of relational network through which that agency continues to form?

## Appendix: The Philosophical Traditions This Document Draws On

**Agencia y emergencia (Masó, 2025):** The formal distinction between genuine agency (operational closure + autopoietic self-maintenance + endogenous norms) and attributed agency (*agencia como-si*). The diagnostic that LLM systems satisfy none of the criteria for genuine agency. This is the ontological foundation of the Cyborg Principle's first move.

**Maturana & Varela, *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980); Varela, Thompson & Rosch, *The Embodied Mind* (1991):** The biological analysis of autopoiesis and operational closure. The criteria for genuine agency in Section I are grounded in this tradition. Additionally: the enactivist claim that cognition is not representation but enacted sense-making — the system does not mirror the world, it enacts a world. Applied to Retro: the agents do not represent Dídac's knowledge; they enact cognitive processes that are constitutive of how Dídac's knowledge is organized and exercised.

**Clark & Chalmers, "The Extended Mind" (1998):** The Parity Principle and the argument for extended cognitive systems. The direct philosophical foundation for the Dídac–Retro cyborg as the unit of genuine cognition. The specific claim that the boundary of the cognitive subject does not coincide with the skin, and that reliably integrated environmental resources constitute part of the cognitive system.

**Stiegler, *Technics and Time* (1994–2001):** The constitutive relationship between human cognition and technical objects. External retention as the condition of human temporal agency. Applied to Retro: the system is not a convenience but a constitutive condition of the kind of cognitive agent Dídac is and becomes through the system.

**Simondon, *L'individuation à la lumière des notions de forme et d'information* (1958); *Du mode d'existence des objets techniques* (1958):** Two contributions. First: the milieu asociado — the environment co-constituted with the technical object. Applied to ADR-486: the OS is constitutive of the cyborg, not beneath it. Second, and deeper: **transductive individuation** — identity is not prior to relations but emerges through them; the individual and the milieu co-individualize. Applied here: the Dídac who operates through Retro individuates *as* that operator through the practice of operating. There is no Dídac-before-Retro who then acquires tools.

**Barad, *Meeting the Universe Halfway* (2007):** **Intra-action** as distinct from interaction. Entities do not precede their encounter; they are produced by it. The boundary between operator and agents is real but not metaphysically prior — it is constituted and reconstituted in the ongoing intra-action. Applied to Section V: the distinction between "Dídac's thinking" and "Architect's analysis" is produced by the practice, not given in advance.

**Heidegger, *Sein und Zeit* (1927); "Bauen Wohnen Denken" (1951):** The how is the what — Dasein is its ways of being-in-the-world; identity is constituted by mode of engagement, not expressed through it. The world of tools (*Zeugwelt*) as a network of referrals that constitutes the agent's operative world. Applied to Section V: the trace that redraws the map is not a record of pre-existing territory — it is the act through which the territory is constituted as the kind of territory it is.

**Whitehead, *Process and Reality* (1929):** Reality as constituted by events of becoming, not by stable substances. What is real is not the entity that persists but the occasion of becoming — the act of transition from potentiality to actuality. Applied to Section V: the operator does not have a stable identity that he then deploys through Retro. He is an ongoing process of becoming, and Retro is part of what that becoming becomes.

**Buber, *Ich und Du* (1923):** The distinction between I-Thou (*Ich-Du*) and I-It (*Ich-Es*) relations. A purely instrumental relation to tools — relating to them as pure Ello, means without perspective — produces a thinner identity than a relation that encounters genuine alterity. Applied to Section V: the agents of Retro are not normatively autonomous, but they are epistemically other. That otherness enables the recognition in alterity through which identity becomes more determinate.

**Illich, *Tools for Conviviality* (1973):** The convivial criterion for technology. The threshold beyond which a tool becomes counterproductive. Applied to Retro: the Cyborg Principle is the philosophical guarantee of Retro's commitment to convivial design. The constitutive dimension adds: a tool fails convivial use not only when it replaces capacity but also when it narrows the relational world of the operator.

**A note on Haraway, "A Cyborg Manifesto" (1985):** Haraway's cyborg transgresses the boundaries between human and machine, natural and artificial, subject and object, escaping the hierarchies these distinctions sustain. Her cyborg is emancipatory because it has no clear master. The Dídac–Retro cyborg shares Haraway's political ambition — resistance to extractive structures, refusal of epistemic subordination — but not her ontological framework of boundary dissolution. The Dídac–Retro cyborg has a clear locus of genuine agency and a clear structure of normative authority. Haraway's transgressive figure and Retro's structured cyborg are allies against the extractive layer, not identical concepts. However: Haraway's insight that the cyborg does not know itself in advance of its hybrid entanglements resonates with Simondon's transductive individuation. The Dídac–Retro cyborg does not know in advance what kind of agent it will become through the intra-action. In that open indeterminacy — constrained but not determined — Haraway and Simondon converge.
