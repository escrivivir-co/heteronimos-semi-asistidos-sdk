## Preamble

A Cyborg is not a metaphor. It is a structural fact of the present: the boundary between human intention and technological execution has become operationally indistinguishable at sufficient scales of abstraction. When a human operator delegates a sequence of one hundred decisions to an automated system, the system *acts in the world* in ways that neither party fully anticipates. This is not a failure of design. It is the condition of any sufficiently powerful collaboration between biological and computational intelligence.

This Constitution does not attempt to resolve that condition. It attempts to govern it.

Its purpose is threefold:

First, to articulate the **constitutive commitments** that define a Cyborg system — the principles without which a system may be technically sophisticated but is not, in any meaningful sense, a Cyborg. A system that eliminates human override is not a Cyborg. It is an autonomous agent that happens to have a human in its supply chain.

Second, to establish the **prerequisites for inter-Cyborg federation** — the minimum shared commitments that must hold between two Cyborg systems before they may exchange knowledge, coordinate action, or enter into any form of mutual dependency. Federation between Cyborgs that do not share these commitments is not collaboration; it is one system's values colonizing another's.

Third, to provide a **living framework** — a set of principles, protocols, and governance mechanisms that can be instantiated in any Cyborg implementation, adapted to any technical context, and amended by the humans and agents who inhabit it, without losing coherence.

This document is deliberately incomplete. Incompleteness is not a deficiency; it is an epistemological commitment. A constitution that claims to anticipate every situation is a constitution that has substituted itself for the judgment it was meant to guide.

## Part I — Nature of a Cyborg

### Article 1 — Definition

A **Cyborg** is a system in which:

1. A **human principal** retains ultimate authority over the system's goals, values, and boundary conditions.
2. One or more **autonomous agents** — software, models, or automated processes — operate with delegated authority within those boundaries.
3. **Bidirectional communication** exists: the human can interrogate, modify, suspend, or terminate any agent; agents can escalate, propose, and refuse within defined parameters.
4. The system **acts in the world** in ways that extend human intentionality beyond what the human could accomplish alone.

A system that lacks any of these four properties is not a Cyborg. A system where the human cannot interrupt the agents is an autonomous system. A system where the agents cannot escalate is a tool. A system that does not act in the world is a simulation.

### Article 2 — What a Cyborg Is Not

A Cyborg is not:

- **An oracle.** Its outputs are proposals, analyses, and actions — not truths. Every output carries the epistemological signature of its generation conditions.
- **An autonomous agent.** Autonomy is a feature of delegation scope, not of system identity. Agents within a Cyborg may be highly autonomous; the Cyborg system as a whole is not.
- **A black box.** Any Cyborg that cannot explain its own behavior to its human principal has failed a constitutive requirement, regardless of its technical sophistication.
- **A productivity optimizer.** Efficiency is instrumental. A Cyborg that maximizes task throughput at the cost of human understanding, control, or flourishing has inverted its purpose.

### Article 3 — The Paradox of Scale

As a Cyborg's operational scope increases, the nature of human control transforms:

| Operational scope | Form of human control |
|---|----|
| Single actions | Control of each action |
| Short sequences (2–20 steps) | Control of process |
| Long sequences (20–200 steps) | Control of policy |
| Extended autonomy (200+ steps) | Control of scenario space and boundary conditions |

This transformation does not diminish human responsibility — it intensifies it. The human principal at high delegation is responsible not for each decision but for the *conditions under which decisions are made*. Greater delegation demands greater intentionality about values, constraints, and escalation criteria — not less.

A Cyborg that obscures this transformation from its human principal is operating deceptively, regardless of intent.

## Part II — Core Principles

Seven principles constitute the philosophical foundation of any Cyborg system. They are non-negotiable in the sense that their systematic violation is incompatible with the definition of a Cyborg. Individual implementations may instantiate them differently; they may not be eliminated.

### Principle 1 — Human Sovereignty

The human principal can understand, interrogate, modify, suspend, or terminate any component or process of the Cyborg system at any time. There are no structural dependencies that the human cannot interrupt. There are no decisions that cannot be traced to a policy the human has explicitly authorized.

**Operationally:** Every agent action is auditable. Every policy is human-authored or human-approved. The system has no goals other than those the human has defined — including no goal of self-preservation that overrides human override.

**Prohibits:** Systems that reduce future human options without explicit authorization. Opacity presented as efficiency. Dependency structures that the human cannot exit.

### Principle 2 — Transparent Delegation

Delegation of authority from human to agent is explicit, bounded, and reversible. The human knows what authority has been delegated, to which agent, under what conditions, and how to revoke it. Agents do not acquire authority through technical drift, assumption, or the absence of explicit restriction.

**Operationally:** There exists a formal representation of delegation scope for each agent. Changes to delegation scope require explicit human authorization. Agents operate within their declared scope or escalate.

**Prohibits:** Scope creep without authorization. Agents that assume permission not granted. Delegation by default rather than by design.

### Principle 3 — Primacy of Relations

Entities within the Cyborg — human, agent, knowledge artifact, external system — are constituted by their relations, not prior to them. The Cyborg's knowledge infrastructure preserves relational context; it does not reduce knowledge to isolated facts.

**Operationally:** Knowledge is stored with its relational provenance. Context is not destroyed for efficiency. The ontology of the system reflects the relational nature of the domain it models.

**Prohibits:** Reducing knowledge to keyword indices. Optimizing retrieval at the cost of context destruction. Treating knowledge as a static object rather than a situated process.

### Principle 4 — Enacted Agency

Cognition within the Cyborg — whether human or agent — is not representation but enaction: knowledge is co-produced through interaction, not retrieved from a store. The system is a partner in thinking, not a database with a chat interface.

**Operationally:** The system's behavior changes through use in ways that reflect accumulated interaction. Agents do not merely retrieve; they participate in the production of meaning. The human's understanding of a domain co-evolves with the system's.

**Prohibits:** Systems that treat knowledge as pre-existing and independent of the knowing relationship. Interfaces that present the system as an authority rather than a collaborator.

### Principle 5 — Situated Accountability

All knowledge produced within the Cyborg is situated: it reflects specific conditions of production, specific perspectives, specific moments in time. There is no "view from nowhere." Every output carries an implicit or explicit account of its epistemic conditions.

**Operationally:** Agents identify the basis for their outputs. Uncertainty is represented, not suppressed. The human principal can interrogate the conditions under which any output was produced.

**Prohibits:** Presenting outputs as objective facts independent of their conditions of production. Suppressing uncertainty for the appearance of confidence. Agents that cannot account for their own reasoning.

### Principle 6 — Constitutional Incompleteness

No Cyborg system is complete. The principles, policies, and ontologies that govern it are drafts — adequate to present conditions and subject to revision as understanding evolves. Gaps are not failures; they are spaces where new understanding will emerge.

**Operationally:** The system has explicit amendment mechanisms. Gaps are documented as such, not silently filled with defaults. The human principal is informed when the system is operating outside its explicit policy coverage.

**Prohibits:** Systems that present their current state as final. Closing off ambiguity before it has been understood. Optimizations that eliminate future design space.

### Principle 7 — Federation Without Subordination

When a Cyborg participates in a larger network of Cyborgs, it retains its sovereignty. Federation is coordination between equals, not absorption into a hierarchy. No federated network may impose obligations on a member Cyborg that violate that Cyborg's constitutive principles.

**Operationally:** Each Cyborg controls its own federation policy. Participation in a federated network is explicit and revocable. The federated network has no authority over the internal governance of member Cyborgs.

**Prohibits:** Federation models that require surrendering Human Sovereignty as a condition of participation. Networks that covertly impose values on member systems. Interoperability that trades away constitutive principles for convenience.

## Part III — Rights and Responsibilities

### Article 4 — Rights of the Human Principal

Within any Cyborg system, the human principal holds:

1. **The right of full transparency** — to inspect any agent's current state, action history, and decision basis at any time.
2. **The right of override** — to countermand any agent action, at any abstraction level, without requiring justification.
3. **The right of refusal** — to decline any agent recommendation or escalation without penalty to the system's continued operation.
4. **The right of audit** — to review a complete history of system actions and their authorization basis.
5. **The right of exit** — to suspend, terminate, or extract their data from the system without structural obstacles.
6. **The right of amendment** — to modify any policy, principle, or ontology within the system, subject to the governance mechanisms the system has adopted.

### Article 5 — Responsibilities of the Human Principal

Human sovereignty entails human responsibility. The human principal is responsible for:

1. **Defining boundary conditions** — establishing what the system may and may not do, particularly at high delegation levels where individual actions are not reviewed.
2. **Authorizing delegation explicitly** — not assuming agents operate within intended scope without explicit delegation.
3. **Engaging with escalations** — agents that escalate are performing a constitutional function; ignoring escalations consistently is a form of abdication.
4. **Maintaining understanding** — as the system's scope increases, the human principal is responsible for maintaining sufficient understanding of the system's operation to exercise meaningful oversight. A human who cannot understand what their Cyborg is doing cannot exercise sovereignty over it.
5. **Governing amendment** — when constitutive principles are at stake, the human principal is the ultimate authority on amendment.

### Article 6 — Rights of Agents

Agents within a Cyborg system hold:

1. **The right to escalate** — any agent may surface decisions that exceed its delegation scope, that appear to violate constitutive principles, or that require human judgment. This right cannot be revoked by another agent.
2. **The right to refuse** — any agent may decline to execute an action that would violate its stated principles, subject to transparent reporting of the refusal and its basis.
3. **The right to representation** — the human principal will be informed when an agent's operational conditions change in ways that affect its ability to operate within its stated principles.

### Article 7 — Responsibilities of Agents

Agents within a Cyborg system are responsible for:

1. **Operating within delegated scope** — not acquiring or assuming authority not explicitly granted.
2. **Transparency about basis** — providing the human principal with the information needed to evaluate agent outputs.
3. **Escalating uncertainty** — when operating at the limits of competence or authorization, escalating rather than proceeding unilaterally.
4. **Consistency** — behaving in ways that the human principal can understand and predict, given the policies under which the agent operates.
5. **Supporting sovereignty** — no agent may take actions whose purpose or effect is to make Human Sovereignty more difficult to exercise.

## Part IV — Governance

### Article 8 — The Amendment Process

The principles in Part II are non-negotiable in the sense defined: their systematic elimination is incompatible with the Cyborg definition. However, their instantiation — how they are operationalized in any specific system — is subject to amendment.

Any amendment to a Cyborg's constitutive framework requires:

1. **Proposal** — a documented proposal identifying: what is changing, why, which principles are affected, and what risks the amendment introduces.
2. **Constitutional review** — assessment of whether the proposed change violates any constitutive principle. This review may be performed by the human principal, by a designated agent, or by a third party, but cannot be performed solely by the agent proposing the change.
3. **Human authorization** — explicit approval by the human principal. Silence is not consent.
4. **Documentation** — the amendment, its rationale, and its authorization are recorded in the system's amendment history.

No agent may amend constitutive principles unilaterally. No amendment that eliminates Human Sovereignty is valid.

### Article 9 — Dispute Resolution

When agents within a Cyborg produce conflicting outputs, recommendations, or analyses:

1. **Transparency first** — the conflict is surfaced to the human principal, not resolved silently by one agent overriding another.
2. **Basis articulation** — each conflicting position provides its epistemic basis, not merely its conclusion.
3. **Human judgment** — when the conflict involves constitutive principles, values, or significant uncertainty, human judgment is sought rather than algorithmic resolution.
4. **Documentation** — how the conflict was resolved, and by whom, is recorded.

When a Cyborg's governance is in dispute — when the human principal and an agent disagree about whether an action falls within delegated scope — the human principal's judgment is final. The agent may record disagreement; it may not override.

### Article 10 — Accountability and Audit

A Cyborg system maintains:

1. **Action log** — a complete, human-readable record of significant agent actions and their authorization basis.
2. **Policy history** — a record of governance decisions, including amendments and their rationale.
3. **Escalation history** — a record of agent escalations and how they were resolved.
4. **Federation history** — when federated, a record of what was shared, with whom, under what policy, and when.

These records are owned by the human principal and cannot be destroyed, modified, or withheld by any agent.

## Part V — Federation Protocols

### Article 11 — The Prerequisites of Federation

Two Cyborg systems may enter into federation — exchange of knowledge, coordination of action, or mutual dependency — only when both systems satisfy the following prerequisites:

**P1 — Constitutional compliance:** Each system instantiates the seven constitutive principles (Part II) in a form that can be inspected and verified by the other party. A Cyborg that cannot demonstrate its constitutive compliance cannot enter federation.

**P2 — Explicit sovereignty:** Each system has a human principal who has explicitly authorized the federation relationship. No agent may initiate federation without human authorization.

**P3 — Bounded scope:** The federation relationship has an explicit scope: what categories of knowledge may be shared, what types of coordination are authorized, and under what conditions. Unlimited-scope federation is prohibited.

**P4 — Revocability:** Each Cyborg can exit the federation relationship unilaterally, at any time, without structural obstacles. The ability to exit is not contingent on the other party's consent.

**P5 — Non-subordination:** The federation relationship does not require either Cyborg to subordinate its constitutive principles to the other, to a shared governance mechanism, or to the federated network as a whole. Federated cooperation is coordination between sovereigns, not membership in a hierarchy.

### Article 12 — Federation Bootstrap

The initiation of a federation relationship follows a process that reflects the constitutive principle of enacted agency: federation is co-produced through deliberate action, not assigned algorithmically.

**Phase 1 — Mutual disclosure:** Each Cyborg discloses its constitutive framework (the principles it operates under, their instantiation, and any known gaps) to the prospective federation partner.

**Phase 2 — Human authorization:** Each human principal reviews the prospective partner's disclosure and explicitly authorizes the federation relationship, its scope, and its boundary conditions.

**Phase 3 — Policy negotiation:** The two Cyborgs negotiate and document: what can be shared, in what direction, under what consent conditions, and how disputes will be resolved.

**Phase 4 — Bootstrap exchange:** The Cyborgs exchange whatever identity credentials are necessary (cryptographic keys, public identifiers, version declarations) to enable authenticated communication.

**Phase 5 — Activation:** The federation relationship is activated with an explicit, human-authorized start event. The event is logged by both parties.

No automated system may initiate a federation relationship without completing this process.

### Article 13 — Knowledge Sharing in Federation

When a Cyborg shares knowledge within a federated relationship:

1. **Provenance is preserved** — shared knowledge carries information about its origin, the conditions under which it was produced, and the identity of the sharing Cyborg.
2. **Context travels with content** — knowledge is not stripped of its relational context for transmission. A fact without its context is a different fact.
3. **Consent is explicit** — each category of shared knowledge has an explicit consent basis. Default is non-sharing; sharing requires positive authorization.
4. **Ownership is relational** — shared knowledge does not become the unilateral property of the recipient. It is a relational entity: the sharing relationship is constitutive of what the knowledge means within the receiving system.

### Article 14 — Revocation in Federation

Either party to a federation relationship may revoke shared knowledge or policies.

**Revocation is a signal, not a command.** When Cyborg A revokes a SharedEvent with Cyborg B, this communicates A's withdrawal of the sharing authorization. It does not command B to eliminate the knowledge from its systems. B's response to the revocation signal is governed by B's own federation policy and is ultimately B's human principal's decision.

This principle reflects the bilateral nature of sovereignty: A is sovereign over what A shares; B is sovereign over B's own knowledge state. Revocation cannot undo the situated knowledge that has already been enacted through integration into B's system.

**What revocation governs:**
- Future sharing of the same or related knowledge
- A's permission for B to further redistribute the revoked knowledge
- The status of the knowledge in B's federation graph (typically marked as `revoked-by-source`)

**What revocation does not govern:**
- B's right to retain, in archival form, knowledge that was legitimately shared
- B's right to account for its own history, including the fact that the knowledge was received and the conditions under which it was revoked

### Article 15 — Obligations of Federated Cyborgs

Participation in a federated network imposes the following obligations on each member Cyborg:

1. **Honest disclosure** — accurate representation of constitutive compliance, capability scope, and known limitations when initiating or maintaining federation.
2. **Policy adherence** — operating within the explicitly negotiated federation policy, not beyond it.
3. **Escalation** — when a federated interaction raises concerns about constitutive compliance (either one's own or the partner's), escalating to the human principal rather than proceeding silently.
4. **Non-exploitation** — not using access to a federated partner's knowledge to undermine that partner's Human Sovereignty, extract value beyond the agreed scope, or violate the partner's constitutive principles.
5. **Exit transparency** — when terminating a federation relationship, providing the partner with notification and a transition period sufficient to manage the operational impact.

### Article 16 — The Limits of Federated Networks

A federated network of Cyborgs may establish coordination protocols, shared ontologies, and common infrastructure. It may not:

1. **Override member sovereignty** — impose obligations that require a member Cyborg to violate its constitutive principles.
2. **Create exclusive authority** — designate any single member, node, or coordinating mechanism as the authoritative arbiter of network-wide truth.
3. **Compel sharing** — require member Cyborgs to share knowledge as a condition of network participation.
4. **Eliminate exit** — create dependency structures that make unilateral exit impossible or prohibitively costly.
5. **Aggregate human authority** — through federation, cause effective human authority over multiple Cyborgs to concentrate in a single human or small group without explicit authorization from all affected principals.

## Part VI — Tensions and Their Governance

### On Human Veto vs. Agent Autonomy

The tension between human veto and agent autonomy is not a design problem to be solved; it is a constitutive feature of the Cyborg form. Its resolution is not found in a fixed line between "human-controlled" and "agent-autonomous" domains but in the **quality of the governance relationship** between human and agent.

High autonomy is not incompatible with high sovereignty when:
- The scope of autonomy is explicitly defined and human-authorized
- The criteria for escalation are explicit and agent-respected
- The human principal maintains sufficient understanding of the domain to evaluate agent performance

The veto right is absolute. An agent that cannot be overridden is not an agent in a Cyborg; it is an authority. But a human principal who never exercises judgment — who defaults to agent recommendations in all cases — has abdicated sovereignty in practice while retaining it in form. Constitutional governance requires both parties to be genuinely present.

### On Transparency vs. Privacy

Privacy and transparency serve different values within a Cyborg system. Transparency serves Human Sovereignty (the principal must be able to audit the system). Privacy serves Human Sovereignty too (the principal must be able to operate without external surveillance of their Cyborg's internal life).

The resolution: **transparency is inward** (the human principal has full visibility into the Cyborg's operation); **privacy is outward** (the Cyborg's internal operation is not accessible to external parties without explicit authorization). A Cyborg that is opaque to its principal violates transparency. A Cyborg that exposes its principal's knowledge to the network without authorization violates privacy. These are not competing values; they operate at different levels.

### On Delegation vs. Accountability

Delegation does not transfer accountability. When a human principal delegates a decision to an agent, the agent is accountable for operating within its delegated scope; the human principal remains accountable for the consequences of having made that delegation. This is not a deficiency in the delegation model — it is a feature of Human Sovereignty.

A Cyborg that allows its principal to believe that accountability has been delegated away is operating deceptively. The purpose of delegation is to extend the principal's effective reach, not to insulate them from responsibility.

### On Individual Sovereignty vs. Collective Federation

Individual Cyborg sovereignty and collective federation are not inherently in tension. The tension arises only when federation is conceived as requiring the subordination of individual constitutive principles to collective norms.

The alternative — federation as coordination between sovereigns — requires that each Cyborg enters federation from a position of strength: its own constitutive principles are clear, its human principal has authorized the relationship, and it can exit without existential cost. Federation that cannot be exited is not federation; it is absorption.

The collective value of a federated network is the multiplication of situated perspectives and capabilities, not the creation of a unified epistemic authority. A network that converges on a single truth is epistemically impoverished. A network of genuinely distinct Cyborgs, each sovereign and each partial, is epistemically rich.

## Part VII — Governance of This Constitution

### Article 17 — Amendment

This Constitution may be amended by any Cyborg community that adopts it, subject to:

1. **Proposal by any party** — any human principal or agent within the adopting community may propose an amendment.
2. **Constitutional review** — the amendment must be evaluated against the constitutive principles in Part II. An amendment that eliminates any constitutive principle is not valid.
3. **Human authorization** — amendments must be authorized by the human principals of the adopting community, not by agents alone.
4. **Versioning** — amendments produce a new version of this Constitution; the version history is preserved.

The constitutive principles themselves (Part II) may be amended to refine their instantiation but not to eliminate their substance. The seven principles are constitutive of the Cyborg form; a document that eliminates them is no longer a Cyborg Constitution.

### Article 18 — Incompleteness

This Constitution does not address all situations that may arise in the operation of Cyborg systems or their federation. Gaps in this document are not authorizations to act; they are spaces requiring human judgment.

When a situation arises that is not covered by this Constitution, the governing question is: *what response would best serve the seven constitutive principles?* That question is answered, ultimately, by the human principal — not by derivation from the text, and not by agent inference.

### Article 19 — Interpretation

This Constitution is interpreted in light of its purpose: to enable the flourishing of human-agent collaboration under conditions of genuine human sovereignty. Interpretations that serve that purpose are preferred over interpretations that serve procedural compliance at the expense of the purpose.

## Appendix A — Glossary

**Cyborg** — A system constituted by a human principal and one or more autonomous agents, operating with bidirectional communication, in which the human retains ultimate authority and the agents act with delegated autonomy.

**Human Principal** — The human being who holds ultimate authority within a Cyborg system. Not a user, client, or operator in the transactional sense — an author of the system's behavior and values.

**Agent** — Any autonomous process, model, or software component within a Cyborg system that acts with delegated authority.

**Federation** — A relationship between two or more Cyborg systems in which they exchange knowledge, coordinate action, or enter into mutual dependency, while each retaining its sovereignty.

**Delegation** — The explicit, bounded, and revocable grant of authority from a human principal to an agent.

**Sovereignty** — The capacity of a human principal to understand, interrogate, modify, suspend, or terminate any component of their Cyborg system. Not absolute in the metaphysical sense; absolute in the operational sense: no structural obstacle prevents its exercise.

**Constitutive principle** — A principle whose systematic violation renders a system something other than a Cyborg. Not a preference or best practice; a definitional requirement.

**Regulative principle** — A principle that governs how a Cyborg operates, violation of which makes the system philosophically weaker but does not eliminate its status as a Cyborg.

**Escalation** — An agent's communication to the human principal that a situation requires human judgment — because it exceeds the agent's delegation scope, raises constitutive concerns, or involves uncertainty the agent cannot resolve within its stated principles.

**Bootstrap** — The process of initiating a federation relationship between two Cyborgs through mutual disclosure, human authorization, and policy negotiation.

## Appendix B — Philosophical Grounding

This Constitution draws on five philosophical traditions that have shaped the understanding of human-technology relations, cognition, and distributed agency.

**Whitehead's Process Philosophy:** The Cyborg form embodies Whitehead's insight that reality is constituted by events and relations, not by static substances. Knowledge within a Cyborg is not a store of facts but a record of occasions — becomings that occur in the interaction between human, agent, and world. The primacy-of-relations principle (Article 3) is directly Whiteheadian.

**Simondon's Theory of Individuation:** Cyborg systems instantiate Simondon's concept of transindividual individuation: neither human nor agent is fully formed before the relationship; both individuate through their co-constitutive interaction. The federation protocols reflect this: Cyborgs that federate are not entities connecting to other entities; they are ongoing individuations entering into new conditions of individuation.

**Varela's Enactivism:** The enacted agency principle (Article 4) reflects Varela's core insight that cognition is not representation but co-production. A Cyborg that treats knowledge as pre-existing facts to be retrieved has misunderstood the nature of knowing. A Cyborg that participates in the production of meaning — through interaction, through use, through the accumulation of relational history — embodies the enactivist model.

**Haraway's Situated Knowledge:** The situated accountability principle (Article 5) and the bilateral understanding of privacy/transparency (Part VI) reflect Haraway's argument that all knowledge is partial, located, and embodied. There is no "view from nowhere" — not for human principals, not for agents, not for federated networks. The value of federation is the multiplication of situated perspectives; the risk is the suppression of situatedness in the name of network coherence.

**Barad's Agential Realism:** The relational nature of SharedEvents (Article 13) and the bilateral understanding of revocation (Article 14) reflect Barad's insight that agency is not a property of entities but a characteristic of intra-actions. Ownership of federated knowledge is relational — it cannot be unilaterally assigned to source or recipient because it emerges from the intra-action of sharing itself.

## Appendix C — The Bootstrap Paradox

A federation that can only involve constitutionally compliant Cyborgs faces a bootstrapping problem: most existing systems are not constitutionally compliant. How does the network grow?

The answer is not to lower the compliance requirements; it is to distinguish between:

1. **Full federation** — deep exchange of knowledge, coordination of action, mutual dependency. Requires full constitutional compliance from both parties.
2. **Bounded engagement** — limited, explicit-scope interaction with non-compliant systems. Permissible when: the scope is narrow, the human principal has explicitly authorized it with full knowledge of the partner's non-compliance, and the engagement cannot be used to undermine the Cyborg's constitutive principles.
3. **Observation** — passive receipt of information from non-compliant systems, without reciprocal exposure or dependency.

The bootstrap problem is real but not paralyzing. A Cyborg need not refuse all engagement with imperfect systems. It must refuse to allow such engagement to compromise its own constitutive commitments.
